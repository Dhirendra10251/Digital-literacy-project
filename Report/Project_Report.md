## Introduction

This report documents my Digital Literacy Portfolio, submitted as part of the CSE0001 course project at VIT Bhopal University. The project is built around the scenario of a Student Digital Ambassador — a student representative who helps peers navigate the digital world responsibly, professionally, and safely.

Over the course of five tasks, I explored different dimensions of digital literacy: creating awareness resources, building a professional online presence, working with coding and collaboration platforms, practising professional digital communication, and researching cybercrime. All tasks are documented in this report and supported by evidence uploaded to my GitHub repository.

---

## Task 1 - Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic

### Tool Used

For this task, I used a digital design tool to create a single-page visual infographic titled "Navigate the Digital World with Confidence."

### What the Infographic Shows

The infographic covers four key topics from the Digital Literacy course:

- **What digital literacy is** - defined as the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information using digital technologies, responsibly and effectively.
- **Useful digital tools for students** - including GitHub, LinkedIn, HackerRank, Google Workspace, and Canva.
- **Safe internet practices** - such as enabling two-factor authentication, never sharing OTPs, verifying links before clicking, and using strong passwords.
- **Professional online presence** - highlighting the importance of maintaining a clean GitHub profile, a complete LinkedIn bio, and responsible social media use.

The design uses a structured card-based layout with colour coding for each topic, making it easy for peers to scan and absorb the information quickly.

### Reflection 

One thing I found particularly interesting while making the infographic was how the process of condensing information into short, visual-friendly points forces you to truly understand the concept first. It is not possible to design a clear infographic about something you do not understand yourself. Working with a design tool also introduced me to the value of visual hierarchy — using size, colour, and spacing to guide the reader's eye across the page in a deliberate order.

The most challenging part was deciding what to leave out. Digital literacy is a broad topic, and narrowing it down to four focused panels required careful prioritisation. I initially wanted to include email etiquette as a fifth panel, but the card became too cluttered, so I focused instead on the four topics that felt most immediately relevant to a first-year student just beginning to build their digital identity. The experience gave me a new appreciation for design as a communication skill, not just an aesthetic one.

## Task 4 - Email Etiquette & Social Media Guide

### Communication Scenario and Key Learnings

For this task, I drafted two professional emails and created a social media conduct checklist. The email-writing process reinforced how much the subject line and opening sentence determine whether a professional email will be read seriously. A vague subject like "Help needed" signals carelessness; a specific one like "Request for Assignment Extension - CSE0001 Digital Literacy" immediately communicates purpose and professionalism.

One situation that illustrates the real consequences of poor digital communication is students publicly posting complaints about faculty or examinations on social media using their real names. In documented cases, this has led to disciplinary proceedings and long-term damage to professional relationships. What could have been done differently is straightforward - applying a three-second pause before posting, and asking: would I be comfortable if my professor or future employer saw this? That single habit prevents most digital communication mistakes.

## Task 5 - Cybercrime Awareness Case Study

### What Surprised Me and One Habit I Will Change

For this task, I researched UPI Payment Fraud and wrote a case study documenting how the crime works, who it targets, and what the consequences are. I also created a 10-point prevention checklist tailored to Indian college students, including UPI-specific safety tips and the official reporting channels — the National Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) and Helpline 1930.

What surprised me most was how non-technical UPI fraud actually is. The attacker does not hack anything — they simply exploit a misunderstanding about how the collect request feature works. The victim authorises the transaction themselves, believing they are receiving money. This was a genuine revelation.

One habit I will personally change as a result: I will never approve a UPI collect request I did not initiate, and I will never scan a QR code in response to a promise of receiving money. These two rules would prevent the majority of such cases.
